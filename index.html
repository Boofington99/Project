My Digital Media Design Journey in South Korea


Exploring design, culture, storytelling, and everyday life through my DMD experience in South Korea.

About The Trip

This website documents my Digital Media & Design experience in South Korea through photography, journal entries, cultural exploration, and design inspiration.


During the summer of 2026, I had the opportunity to travel to South Korea through the University of Connecticut's Digital Media & Design program. Throughout this month-long experience, I explored Korean culture, design, technology, museums, architecture, and everyday life while documenting my journey through photography and journal entries. This website serves as a collection of my experiences, reflections, and discoveries during my time abroad. From visits to museums and design studios to exploring Seoul's neighborhoods, food, and culture, each entry captures a different aspect of the trip. My goal is to share what I learned, how the experience influenced my perspective as a designer, and the memories I created throughout this unique opportunity.

My Daily Journal

When coming to Korea we were task to create a journal to document our experience. This journal is a collection of my thoughts, feelings, and reflections during my time in South Korea. It captures the highs and lows, the challenges and triumphs, and everything in between. Through this journal, I hope to share my journey and provide insights into the transformative power of travel and cultural immersion.

Week 0 (Early Days)
May 11th - May 17th

Entire Week wrap up

Getting here early was a great idea. I had the opportunity to explore the area around us and become familiar with the environment. Adjusting emotionally has been more difficult, though. I miss my family, and I have never been this far away from them before. However, being here with my friend has made the transition easier.

We have been able to visit new places almost every day and have had a great time doing so. Ending the week by exploring more areas, including additional shopping malls, was a lot of fun. I am very excited to start the official week of the school program and meet everyone who will be participating. I am especially looking forward to the welcome dinner. I enjoy trying new foods, so I think this will be a great experience to share with everyone.

Week 1
May 18th - May 24th

Bukchon Hanok village - Tuesday

The Hanbok has changed over time while continuing to represent Korean culture and tradition. Different styles of Hanbok are worn for different occasions, including coming-of-age ceremonies, weddings, and other important stages of life.

Visiting the area was an amazing experience. Being able to wear a Hanbok was a lot of fun, and getting to choose our own outfit made the experience feel even more unique. I especially liked the one I selected because of its bright colors and soft fabric. As we walked around, I noticed an interesting mix of modern and traditional architecture. It was funny to see historic homes and buildings standing alongside brand-new cars parked outside. One of my favorite moments was viewing the area from above and seeing all the buildings, homes, and people walking around in their Hanboks. It gave me a greater appreciation for how Korean traditions continue to exist alongside modern life.

Seodaemun prison history hall and HI KR Ground - Wednesday

Visiting the prison was a very emotional experience. Hearing the stories of what people went through was difficult and painful to think about. I was grateful to have my professor and our guide there, as they helped provide support and context throughout the visit. Although it was emotionally challenging, I learned a great deal about an important part of Korean history. The rainy weather also added to the somber atmosphere of the experience.

Later in the day, we all decided to have lunch together, which was a lot of fun. It was a great opportunity to spend time with everyone outside of our scheduled activities, and I hope we have more chances like this throughout the program.

We ended the day by visiting HiKR Ground. It was a fun and colorful experience with so much to see and explore. There were interactive rooms filled with music and changing lights, as well as a DJ setup that created an exciting atmosphere. The visit was a major contrast to the emotional experience of the morning. Both locations were unique in their own way and offered different perspectives, making the day memorable from beginning to end.

Gyeongbokgung palace and guard change ceremony and National folk museum - Thursday

We first witnessed the changing of the guard ceremony at the palace. The guards marched out carrying huge flags and wearing brightly colored uniforms, which made the ceremony feel both exciting and formal at the same time. Even though their role was to protect the palace, their outfits were full of color and detail. Some of the guards even wore long feathers on their hats. The large flags were especially interesting because they displayed dragon designs. One flag was red with yellow accents, while another was black with red accents, making them stand out against the palace background.

During the visit, we also learned about important parts of Korean history and culture. We saw the 10,000 won bill and learned about the respected scholar featured on it, who was known as a genius in both literature and science. Another interesting fact was how traditional homes used floor heating systems, so people would sleep on mattresses placed directly on the floor to stay warm. We also learned about royal family traditions, including how the king’s mother and grandmother would choose a princess to become his wife through an arranged marriage. If the wife did not have a son, an heir could be chosen from one of the concubines instead.

The scenery around the palace was beautiful as well, especially with the mountain located behind the castle. It created a peaceful view and added to the atmosphere of the historic site. Later, we visited an exhibition about everyday people’s lives throughout history. My favorite part was the Christmas section, where classic American Christmas music was playing. It was unexpected but fun to hear familiar songs while learning about Korean culture and history.

Herb medicine museums and Dondaemon plaza - Friday

Today started with a visit to a medical museum, and one thing I definitely did not expect was getting a foot bath. It was funny going from learning about the history of medicine to relaxing in warm water, but it made the experience really memorable. Afterward, we got body massages, which was something I never fully understood before trying myself. It was a fun and unexpected way to start the morning.

After lunch, we explored Dongdaemun Design Plaza (DDP), designed by Zaha Hadid. The building is known for its smooth, curved design and futuristic style. I learned that over 2,000 relics were found while rebuilding the area, showing how the plaza connects the past with the future.

Some of the most interesting details were that every exterior panel on the building is unique, 40% of the roof is covered with plants, and the air conditioning is built into the floor to save energy. DDP also has multiple entrances instead of one main gate, making the space feel very open and connected. Overall, it was amazing to see such creative architecture in person and learn how thoughtfully the entire plaza was designed.

Week 2
May 25th - May 31st

Naksan Park - Tuesday

After having to change our plans, we ended up going back to the original plan because of the weather. We went to Naksan Park. It was such a nice, peaceful hike with beautiful scenery and views of the neighborhoods below us. The heat wasn’t horrible, but it was definitely starting to get to me. Thankfully, the breeze was incredibly refreshing and made the hike much more enjoyable.

We stopped at a café midway through the hike for a short rest. It was very peaceful and wonderfully cool inside, which was amazing after being out in the heat. The café also provided a great view of the cityscape. Unfortunately, it was very cloudy, but that also helped since we didn’t have the sun beating down on us the entire time.

Getting to see the city from above was amazing, and it made me excited to visit other places with incredible views. After a walk like this, I’m definitely ready for a nap.

Kimchi museum, Tteok Museum, Seoul Museum of Folk Music - Wednesday

We started the day at the Kimchi Museum, where we had the opportunity to make our own kimchi. It was a very fun and exciting experience. Most of the ingredients were already prepared for us, but it was still surprisingly easy and enjoyable to put everything together and jar it. Now comes the long part—waiting for it to ferment! Kimchi is known for being good for gut health, so it will be interesting to enjoy it later and see how it turns out.

After a short lunch, we headed to the Tteok Museum. This museum showcases many different foods made from rice. I had no idea there were so many varieties or that they played such an important role in different ceremonies and traditions. The exhibits walked us through the history of how these foods are crafted, prepared, and used. There was even a mortar and pestle on display to demonstrate how rice was processed. The museum took us from the 1400s all the way to the present day, showing the many possibilities and uses of rice throughout Korean history.

We finished the day at the Seoul Museum of Folk Music. One of my favorite parts was a listening station where visitors could hear recordings of traditional folk songs. They sounded very unique to me, but definitely fit the style of folk music. It was fascinating to hear songs that people sang while working, as well as simple lullabies. Each song told a story from the past, and every one was different from the last. One song even described the history of looms and hemp weaving. It was really interesting to see how history, traditions, and everyday activities have been passed down through the power of music.

Hyundai Motor Studio - Thursday

Today we visited the Hyundai Motorstudio and learned about how Hyundai vehicles are designed, built, and tested before being delivered to customers around the world. The experience began with viewing finished vehicles and then following the entire production process step by step.

One of the most interesting parts was learning how old vehicle scrap can be recycled and reused to help build new cars. We also saw welding robots that use advanced AI technology to maintain a stable temperature while working, making the process more accurate and efficient. The painting technology was also incredibly precise, and we learned that the complete painting process can take up to 20 hours to finish.

Another impressive part of the visit was seeing how machines and robotic systems safely move heavy vehicle parts throughout production. Safety was a major focus throughout the studio. We learned that airbags deploy faster than the blink of an eye, and Hyundai conducts extensive crash tests to help ensure customer safety. The crash-test dummies contain numerous sensors that collect data during testing, allowing engineers to improve vehicle safety features.

The studio also focused on environmental issues and sustainability. We learned that transportation accounts for about 14% of global carbon emissions, which is why Hyundai is researching cleaner technologies for the future. One of the coolest innovations we saw was technology designed to help remove pollutants from the air. Overall, the Hyundai Motorstudio was both educational and fascinating because it demonstrated how technology, safety, and environmental responsibility all come together in modern vehicle production.

National Museum of Korea, Netmarble Game Museum - Friday

Today we visited the National Museum of Korea. The museum had many different sections covering Korea’s history and culture across different time periods. We saw well-preserved artifacts such as slender bronze daggers, porcelain crafts, Roman marble sculptures, and Islamic art. One interesting part was hearing the sound of the Buddhist bell, which was meant to carry the Buddha’s voice far away. The final floor also included a world art and mythology exhibition.

After that, we went to the Netmarble Game Museum. We learned that games date all the way back to ancient civilizations like Mesopotamia and Egypt, and later evolved into modern video games with the creation of computers. The museum also showed the history of gaming consoles and handheld systems from companies like Nintendo and Sega. One of the most fun parts was being able to play arcade games from the 1980s and 1990s.

Week 3
June 1st - June 7th

King Sejong and Admiral Yi Sun-sin - Tuesday

Today we visited The Story of King Sejong and The Story of Admiral Yi Sun-sin. I learned that King Sejong was one of Korea’s greatest rulers during the Joseon Dynasty. He was dedicated to improving the lives of his people and is best known for creating Hangul, the Korean alphabet. During his reign, there were many cultural and scientific advancements, including the development of rain gauges and sundials. He also supported healthcare improvements and believed in fair treatment for women and children.

We also learned about Admiral Yi Sun-sin, one of Korea’s most respected heroes. He was a naval officer who famously defended Korea with only 12 ships against a much larger enemy fleet. Admiral Yi never lost a battle and is remembered for his leadership, courage, and the creation of the turtle ship, which changed naval warfare. Even today, his legacy lives on throughout Korea, and he continues to inspire people with his wisdom and determination.

After the exhibits, we participated in calligraphy and keychain-making activities. During calligraphy, I wrote my name in Korean using a brush and ink. It was exciting to learn the techniques, and the smooth movement of the brush on the paper was very relaxing. Afterward, we made keychains by coloring designs and having them turned into souvenirs. It was a simple but calming activity that allowed us to be creative while enjoying the experience.

MBC Ilsan Drama Studio - Wednesday

Today we visited the MBC Ilsan Drama Studio, and it was fascinating to see how Korean television dramas are produced. The studio was much larger than I expected, yet surprisingly quiet despite the number of people working there. We learned that several episodes can be filmed in a single day and are usually released about two weeks later. It was interesting to hear about the production process, including how different script colors are used for each episode, how five cameras capture scenes from multiple angles, and how actors often spend one to two hours getting their makeup done before filming. I was also surprised to learn that the company prepares gifts for actors to encourage them and that scriptwriters are present during the first day of rehearsals.

We also learned about a drama called First Man, which was released on December 15, 2025. The show focuses on themes of revenge and follows some common story elements often seen in Korean dramas. Watching clips and learning about the filming techniques was especially interesting because of the dramatic camera zooms and close-up shots used to build tension. Overall, the visit gave me a much greater appreciation for the amount of work, planning, and teamwork that goes into creating a television drama.

Guest Speaker and Museum Visit - Thursday

Today we had the opportunity to hear from guest speaker Chansoo Kim, a professor at Hongdae University who has worked in both Hollywood and the Korean film industry. He shared his experiences in animation and visual effects, including his work on Transformers: Dark of the Moon. One of the most interesting topics was how the success of Jurassic Park demonstrated the economic and cultural power of the film industry, helping change the way Korea viewed media from simply art and culture to a major industry. We also learned about important developments that helped create the Korean Wave, including the revision of film laws in the 1990s, the launch of the Busan International Film Festival, the rise of blockbuster films such as Shiri, and the growth of major cinema chains. His presentation showed how Korean film and media have grown into a global force that now influences culture, entertainment, food, beauty, and more around the world.

Later, we visited the National Museum of Korea’s Contemporary History exhibit, where we explored Korea’s journey from the end of World War II to the present day. The museum highlighted Korea’s struggles for freedom, democracy, and economic development, as well as the sacrifices made by previous generations to build the modern Republic of Korea. I found it especially interesting to learn how American military jeeps left after the war were repurposed into vehicles for everyday use and how many people moved to Seoul in search of education and employment during periods of rapid growth. The exhibits also showed changes in daily life, entertainment, and technology over time, giving me a greater appreciation for how much Korea has transformed within a relatively short period. Overall, both the lecture and the museum visit helped me better understand the connection between Korea’s history, culture, and global influence today.

Namsangol Hanok Village - Friday

Today we visited Namsangol Hanok Village, a beautiful historical area that provided a glimpse into traditional Korean life. Although the village is relatively small, it felt very peaceful and was filled with interesting details. One of the highlights was participating in a magnet-making activity. The process required patience and careful planning because it was easy to make mistakes if you rushed. After placing the design onto a special liquid, the magnets developed a shiny finish and were left to dry. I enjoyed creating something with my own hands and learning about the craftsmanship involved in the process.

As we explored the village, I noticed how the size of each house reflected the social status of its former residents. The buildings were small but surprisingly open, allowing visitors to walk through and see how people once lived. Unfortunately, an event was being set up during our visit, which made it more difficult to fully explore some areas. Despite that, I enjoyed the peaceful walking trails, hidden waterfalls, traditional music playing from a nearby café, and a small gallery displaying traditional Korean instruments. We also saw a large time capsule containing items and messages from different parts of the world. While I wish we had been able to experience more of the village, it was still an enjoyable visit and a relaxing way to spend the day.

Week 4
June 8th - June 14th

War Merorial Museum - Tuesday

Today we visited the War Memorial Museum of Korea and learned about the Korean War, which lasted from 1950 to 1953. One of the most interesting things I learned was that the conflict was not just between Korean people, but was heavily influenced by Cold War tensions between larger world powers. After Japan’s occupation of Korea ended following World War II, the peninsula was divided into Soviet-controlled and American-controlled zones, which eventually led to war. Although an armistice was signed in 1953, the war technically never ended, and the DMZ still separates North and South Korea today. The museum displayed many artifacts from the war, including uniforms, weapons, military documents, and other historical items. I was especially moved by the stories of families who were separated by the conflict and never reunited. Learning about the experiences of both soldiers and civilians helped me better understand the sacrifices made during the war. Overall, the visit was both educational and emotional, and it gave me a greater appreciation for Korea’s history.

Korean Broadcasting System (KBS) - Wednesday

Today we toured KBS, the largest broadcasting studio in Korea and the country’s first broadcasting network. It was interesting to learn how KBS operates as a government-run broadcaster and to see the scale of its facilities within Korea’s broadcasting district. During the tour, we were able to watch live radio hosts at work, view television studios recording current programs, and observe a live set from above while a show was being prepared for broadcast. The exhibits also explained the entire production process, from filming to post-production, and demonstrated how AI technology can track individual performers during dance recordings. I especially enjoyed seeing the animation section, where visitors could experience dubbing an animated film, as well as viewing real filming equipment and the evolution of media formats over time. Overall, the tour gave me a greater appreciation for the amount of technology and coordination required to produce modern television and media.

Culture

Throughout my time in South Korea, I experienced many cultural differences that highlighted the importance of respect, community, and consideration for others. Public transportation was designed with accessibility in mind, including priority seating areas for pregnant women and older adults. Many public spaces displayed signs encouraging people to remain quiet, helping create a calm environment for everyone. I also noticed how people often stood to one side of escalators and stairways, allowing those in a hurry to pass. Small details like these demonstrated how everyday routines are shaped by a strong awareness of others.



I also learned about the impact of Korean media, technology, and public life. Korea's film industry has become highly influential both domestically and internationally, and it was interesting to see how movies were adapted for Korean audiences, including changes from English text to Korean text within animations. During our stay, I learned that Election Day is treated as an important civic event, with many people receiving time off from work to vote. I also observed safety features throughout the subway system, including platform screen doors that separate passengers from the tracks. These experiences helped me better understand how Korean society balances innovation, safety, and civic responsibility while maintaining strong cultural traditions.



Reflection

Studying abroad in South Korea was one of the most rewarding experiences of my life. Beyond learning about design, culture, and technology, the experience helped me grow as a person. Being away from home for an extended period made me realize how important my friends and family are to me and how much I value the support they provide. While I enjoyed exploring a new country and gaining independence, the experience gave me a deeper appreciation for the relationships I have back home. I returned with new perspectives, lasting memories, and a greater understanding of both another culture and myself.
